with pericarditis NOTE #1: Myocarditis could mimic this ECG, but normal cath, troponins, echo, and MRI ruled it out. NOTE #2: No pericardial friction rub reported (which would confirm diagnosis). NOTE #3: Pain not described as pleuritic or positional (pain was “constant” and “stabbing”). * Acute MI is far more common than pericarditis in ED settings. * Missing an MI is far more dangerous than missing pericarditis. * This case illustrates appropriate activation of the cath lab. * It expands understanding of atypical ECG presentations of pericarditis. * It is a reminder that patients do not always present in textbook fashion.
