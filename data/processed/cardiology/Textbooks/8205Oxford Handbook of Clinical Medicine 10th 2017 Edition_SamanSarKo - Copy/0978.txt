Because micro-organisms kill our friends we think of them as bad: ‘I have no philosophy, nor piety, no art of refl ection, no theory of compensa- tion to meet things so hide ous, so cruel, and so mad, they are just unspeak- ably horrible and irremediable to me and I stare at them with angry and almost blighted eyes.’ Henry James, 1915, describing the death of Rupert Brooke from septicaemia. But this is a mistake. Kill off micro-organisms and the whole show fi zzles out. Micro-organisms gave us the DNA and organelles needed for reading and digesting this page. Even killing a single pathogen might be a mistake: Sod’s Law will prob- ably ensure that something worse will come to inhabit the vacated ecospace. Prod one part of the system and events ripple out in an unending stream of unintended consequences, played out under the stars, which themselves are evolving, and which donate and receive our primordial elements. Can we win against infectious diseases? No. But winning or losing is the wrong image: infectious diseases have made us who we are. All we can do is live with them.
