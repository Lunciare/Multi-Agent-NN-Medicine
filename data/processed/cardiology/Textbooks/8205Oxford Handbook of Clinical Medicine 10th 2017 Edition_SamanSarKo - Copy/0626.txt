Leucoplakia (fi g 6.2) Is an oral mucosal white patch that will not rub off and is not attributable to any other known disease. It is a premalignant lesion, with a transformation rate, which ranges from 0.6% to 18%. Oral hairy leucoplakia is a shaggy white patch on the side of the tongue seen in HIV, caused by EBV. When in doubt, refer all intra-oral white lesions (see BOX). Aphthous ulcers (fi g 6.3) 20% of us get these shallow, painful ulcers on the tongue or oral mucosa that heal without scarring. Causes of severe ulcers: Crohn’s and coeliac disease; Behçet’s (p694); trauma; erythema multiforme; lichen planus; pemphigus; pemphigoid; infections (herpes simplex, syphilis, Vincent’s angina, p712). : Minor ulcers: avoid oral trauma (eg hard toothbrushes or foods such as toast) and acidic foods or drinks.
