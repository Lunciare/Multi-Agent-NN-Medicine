If the consequences are not clear and the patient has capacity to give consent, you should seek informed written consent as a record of your conversation. For consent to be valid • It can be given any time before the intervention/treatment is initiated. Earlier is better as this will give the patient time to think about the risks, benefi ts, and al- ternatives—he or she may even bring forward questions on issues that you had not considered relevant. Think of consent as an ongoing process throughout the patient’s time with you, not just the moment of signing the form. • The proposed treatment or test must be clearly understood by the patient, taking into account the benefi ts, risks (including complication rates if known), additional procedures, alternative courses of action, and their consequences. • It must be given voluntarily.
