Sebaceous cysts Refer to either epidermal (fi g 13.11) or pilar cysts (they are not of sebaceous origin and contain keratin, not sebum). They appear as fi rm, round, mobile subcutaneous nodules of varying size. Look for the characteristic central punctum. Infection is quite common, and foul pus exits through the punctum. They are com- mon on the scalp, face, neck, and trunk. Treatment: Excision of cyst and contents. Lymph nodes Causes of enlargement: Infection: Glandular fever; brucellosis; TB; HIV; toxoplasmosis; actinomycosis; syphilis. Infi ltration: Malignancy (carcinoma, lymphoma); sarcoidosis. Below the waist, faecal organisms are common (aerobes and anaerobes). Treatment: Incise and drain. Boils (furuncles) are ab- scesses involving a hair follicle and associated glands.
