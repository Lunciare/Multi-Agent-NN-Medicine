It is comprised of elements including meaning, purpose, and something greater than ‘self’. It is distinct from faith, which is a religious experience, that may or may not be part of spirituality. Spiritual pain20,21 or suff ering is common when people are facing death. It can include feelings of hopelessness, guilt, isolation, meaninglessness, and confusion. Consider: • the past: painful memories, guilt • the present: isolation, anger • the future: fear, hopelessness. Reminiscence helps address the past, provides context, and off ers recognition of the patient as an individual. Anger should be acknowledged. Fear of the imagined future may not change, but is potentially reduced through discussion. The nature of hope may need to be modifi ed. If hope for a cure is inappropriate, it should not be the main or only hope. Realistic hopes include discharge from hospital, seeing family members happy, being remembered.
