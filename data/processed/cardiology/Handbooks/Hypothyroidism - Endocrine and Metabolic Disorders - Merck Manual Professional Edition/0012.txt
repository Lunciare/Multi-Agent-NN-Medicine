Geriatrics Essentials: Hypothyroidism Hypothyroidism is particularly common among older adults. It occurs in close to 10% of women and 6% of men > 65 years. Although typically easy to diagnose in younger adults, hypothyroidism may be subtle and manifest atypically in older adults. Older patients have significantly fewer symptoms than do younger patients, and symptoms and signs are often subtle and vague. Many older patients with hypothyroidism present with nonspecific geriatric syndromesâ€”confusion, anorexia, weight loss, falling, incontinence, and decreased mobility. Musculoskeletal symptoms (especially arthralgias) occur often, but arthritis is rare. Muscular aches and weakness, often mimicking polymyalgia rheumatica or polymyositis, and an elevated creatine kinase (CK) level may occur. In older patients, hypothyroidism may mimic dementia or parkinsonism. In older patients, levothyroxine therapy is begun with low doses, usually 25 mcg once a day. Maintenance doses may also need to be lower in older patients. Key Points Hypothyroidism is deficiency of thyroid hormone.
